1. Background & Evolution

From MRI to MRS – The evolution of molecular-level imaging

Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) originated from the principles of Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR), discovered in the 1940s. While MRI revolutionized anatomical imaging, MRS extended its capability to study tissue biochemistry. It enables non-invasive detection of key metabolites like N-acetyl aspartate (NAA), choline, and creatine — aiding early diagnosis of disorders such as brain tumors, epilepsy, and metabolic diseases.

3. Working Mechanism (Simplified)

How Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy works

MRS is based on the same physical principles as MRI but focuses on frequency shifts of specific nuclei (e.g., ¹H, ³¹P). Each metabolite resonates at a unique frequency depending on its chemical environment. The acquired signal is converted to a spectrum via Fourier Transform, showing peaks that correspond to metabolite concentrations.

  • Localized using voxel selection (PRESS/STEAM sequences)
  • Signal captured from hydrogen or phosphorus nuclei
  • Post-processed to obtain metabolite spectrum

4. Instrumentation Setup

Key components of an MRS system

MRI/MRS scanner setup and components

  • Superconducting Magnet – Provides static field (1.5T–7T)
  • Gradient Coils – Spatial localization of voxel
  • RF Coils – Transmission and reception of MR signals
  • Spectrometer Console – Controls pulse sequence and data acquisition
  • Workstation – Processes spectra and quantifies metabolites

5. Applications

Clinical and research relevance of MRS

  • Neurological disorders: brain tumors, epilepsy, multiple sclerosis
  • Metabolic diseases and mitochondrial dysfunction
  • Oncology: distinguishing benign vs malignant lesions
  • Research: neurochemistry and pharmacological response analysis
